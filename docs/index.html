The Messages

Dress Codes

Are Sending

Amber Thomas

Feb 2019

with data collection assistance from Kait Thomas and Anna Houston

If you aren't currently in high school, it's probably been a while since you've read a student handbook. Over the past few months, my colleagues and I have pored over 483 of them from schools across the US. We focused specifically on the dress code section — a set of policies hotly debated for being racist, sexist, and promoting sexualization. This is a story of why we did this and what we found along the way.

In early 2017, writer Dana Schwartz posed a question to the Twitter-verse: "Ladies, when was the first time you were made to feel embarrassed and sexualized for what you wore?"

Ladies, when was the first time you were made to feel embarrassed and sexualized for what you wore? I was in 5th grade, shorts too short.

— Dana Schwartz (@DanaSchwartzzz) March 26, 2017

I thought of my earliest memories of this type of behavior — the times when I wasnt aiming to be sexy, but was perceived that way anyway. I remembered the girls stopped outside of our middle school dance because their shoulders and underarms were bare. Being handed a detention slip because a small sliver of my torso was exposed. Like Dana, each of my earliest memories of feeling sexualized came from the adults that were trying to enforce my schools dress code.

I like to imagine that this wasnt done maliciously. I mean, its not like my schools policy literally said anything like this:

Messages

now

(Not a Real) Dress Code

Ladies, your bodies are too sexy and need to be covered up.

But that doesnt mean that I hadnt still received that message. Like so much else in life, words can often hold hidden meanings or insinuations.

When dealing with only the written word, humans and computers are both pretty terrible at separating out the text from their underlying meanings. Its why many online users have started using the /s notation to indicate that their words should be taken sarcastically, not literally.

All of this makes analyzing dress codes a challenge. Following the lead of researchers studying dress codes in California and Washington DC, we collected data from hundreds of high schools to learn more about what they prohibit and why these policies exist. Interpreting the hidden messages the policies send, however, is trickier. Well rely on research and reports to help add some context to what we found.

Body Parts to Hide

Although the name may imply that dress codes are merely involved with regulating the clothes that students may wear, we found that 80% of schools policies specifically prohibited certain body parts from being visible. Those phrases often looked something like this:

Messages

now

Longmont High School (CO)

[Students may not wear] clothing that bares or exposes traditionally private parts of the body including, but not limited to, the stomach, buttocks, back and breasts.

Messages

now

Teague High School (TX)

Shirts must cover the midriff and must cover the chest area (cleavage must be covered).

Policies like these have recently come under scrutiny. According to opponents, at best, by prohibiting certain body parts from being visible, students receive the message that those body parts are bad, should be hidden, or are of importance to others. At worst, some say that dress codes go so far as to turn whole people into a collection of inappropriate body parts to cover.

According to our analysis, these are the body parts that schools think are most in need of covering.

I wont lie, I was expecting something like genitals to be number one here, but only 6 schools (1%) explicitly ban them. Midriffs, on the other hand — the front part of your body between your chest and your waist — is the most banned body part by a long shot. Which raises the question: why?

The stomach and belly button are not inherently sexual body parts. Though, in Western culture, the midriff, and navel in particular, has a long history of being seen as taboo or indecent. Specifically, the female midriff. Women couldnt show their belly buttons on television until the 1970s or the streets of New York City until 1985. Arguments go on about whether baring the female midriff is fashionable, erotic, or attractive to those looking at her, but always seem to focus on the people that are observing someone elses midriff, rather than the person bearing their own.

This practice of commenting on someone elses body and imposing sexuality upon them (whether they intended to be sexy or not) falls squarely into the American Psychological Associations (APA) definition of sexualization. Compounding the issue, some argue, is that by telling and showing students that these body parts are important to others, we are reinforcing the idea that those parts should be important to them too. Unfortunately, studies have shown that when people are consciously thinking about their appearance, they perform worse on various cognitive tasks like math tests and are more likely to experience eating disorders, low self-esteem, and depression.

Clothes We Wear

Beyond hiding specific body parts, dress code policies typically list a number of items that students are not allowed to wear to school. The average schools dress code prohibits around 32 things (the maximum was at 97). Here are some snippets of what these policies may look like:

Messages

now

McLouth High School (KS)

Shorts and skirt length must be as long, or longer than, the longest fingertip. Shorts and skirts that pass this test may still be deemed inappropriate.

Messages

now

Madras High School (OR)

Low-cut tops, spaghetti straps, halter tops, backless tops, tube tops, A-shirts (muscle shirts), clothing that allows for bare midriffs, low necklines, and/or short shorts are not allowed.

Many schools policies range from prohibiting bare feet or hats to spiked jewelry or promotions of alcohol. Lets focus specifically on clothing items that are worn on your body. This includes any form of shirt, pants, shorts, skirts, dresses, and undergarments.

Although these rules are typically written to apply to all students evenly, in practice, many of them affect some students more than others. For instance, although male students are also not allowed to wear halter tops, that fashion item is typically made for and marketed to girls.

Lets see which of the banned clothing items are specifically marketed to girls, boys, or students of any gender.

Here weve arranged all of the clothing items based on how frequently they are banned.

Youll find short shorts and skirts at the highest end alongside visible underwear.

On the opposite end, youll find items banned in fewer schools like spandex clothing, gym shorts, and the absence of underwear.

You may have noticed that almost all of these items are likely prohibited because they reveal or accentuate parts of the body. In fact, there are only 3 (pajamas, loose clothing, and cutoff pants) that seem to be banned for some other reason.

Of the remaining 35 items, 59% are marketed to girls, 35% to any gender, and only 6% are marketed to boys. Once again, we see that the bodies and clothes that girls wear are more highly regulated than other students.

Note: * the definition of short varies from school to school. The most common lengths for shorts, skirts, and dresses were to the students fingertips (47%), longer than mid-thigh (32%), and longer than the knee (8%). Another 33% didnt specify a length, just prohibited short items of clothing. Short shirts refers typically to shirts that cant be tucked in or touch the top of the pants.

Some people posit that girls clothes are more regulated because boys have fewer clothing options. Afterall, their shirts, pants, and shorts are all largely the same style.

Girls clothing, on the other hand, has a wide variety of style options, a much smaller proportion of which are allowed in many of the schools we surveyed. In a study of clothes available for girls (sizes 8-14) in online stores, 41% of girls clothing in tween stores like Abercrombie Kids, were considered sexualized (and would likely not meet the requirements of most dress codes). While I havent found a similar study on clothes sized for high school students, I cant imagine that clothes for older teens are less sexualized than clothes for their middle-school peers. If youre looking for personal anecdotes of frustrated teens and parents who cant find clothes to satisfy the dress code requirements, you dont have to look far. One mother even snarkily invited the schools principal to take her daughter shopping since she had had no luck in finding shorts that were fingertip length and within budget.

The Words We Tell Our Students

Even after dress codes ban body parts and clothes that expose the body, making it clear that some parts of you should be hidden, the words we use further reinforce this idea. Most dress codes begin with a few sentences informing the reader what a dress code is for and why it exists. Here are a few examples:

Messages

now

Illinois Valley High School (OR)

Bizarre, immodest or revealing clothing demeans the value of the wearer and disrupts the educational atmosphere. It will not be permitted.

Messages

now

Lehigh Senior High School (FL)

The purpose of the Dress Code is to encourage students to focus on the learning process without the distractions of unsuitable dress and grooming.

Messages

now

Wausaukee High (WI)

Students are encouraged to ask themselves the following questions when choosing what to wear to school:
Does my outfit promote modesty and respectability?
Does my choice of clothing make those around me uncomfortable?
Does my clothing expose too much?

Messages

now

Rusk High School (TX)

The districts dress code is established to teach grooming and hygiene, prevent disruption, and minimize safety hazards.

Our words matter. Whether intentionally or not, some of these dress codes are literally telling students that their bodies and modes of dress are distractions or linked to their value or self worth. And as weve already seen, it is largely the female students that are receiving these messages.

We extracted the most common words used to offer any rationale for the dress code policies. Explore some examples of the most common words and phrases below.

Policies using phrases like distract or disrupt are in the vast majority, representing 76% of the schools in our dataset. The phrasing has not gone unnoticed by students. Back in 2014, students began using the hashtag #IAmNotADistraction in retaliation of policies just like these. Afterall, when girls are reprimanded for being distracting they are likely not distracting themselves. The typical argument falls that they are distracting their male classmates and, disturbingly, sometimes adult men.

Some suggest that creating dress codes like the ones weve seen sends a complex message to all students. Specifically, it relays to girls that they are responsible for the way that others see them (and continuing that line of logic, what others do to them) and it relays to boys that they are not in control of their own actions and that they can pass judgement on others based on their appearance.

The words used in dress codes are often incredibly vague. What one administrator may deem appropriate would be cause for violation through the eyes of another. When it comes to the actual enforcement of these rules, 50% of schools specifically state that the final determination in whether or not a student is breaking the dress code will be a subjective one. Those clauses often look like this:

Messages

now

Douglas High School (MA)

Any clothing or personal item that, in the judgment of the administration, interferes with or disrupts the educational process will not be allowed.

Messages

now

Girard High School (PA)

Decency, as interpreted by the administration and staff, is to be maintained at all time.

Bringing subjectivity into this makes something tricky even more complicated because it brings along bias. Reports indicate that students that are curvy or busty are reprimanded more than their less-curvy classmates. Similarly, girls of color, whom are stereotypically perceived as hypersexualized, provocative, and not ladylike are disproportionately targeted. They are found in violation of their schools dress codes more often than their white classmates, even when both were breaking the rules.

So What Do We Do?

While the students leading these protests have often gained the support of their parents, some are wondering where this fight ends. Author, Melissa Schorr asks: [I]s the end game to achieve equality by wearing the skimpiest clothes? Reflexively fighting against all dress code violations brushes aside the reality that there is a time and place for appropriate clothing.

Research has shown that when women wear clothes that are revealing, leave a few buttons undone on her blouse, or dont dress according to accepted societal dress codes, they are viewed as less competent, intelligent, and powerful than other women. Another study found that revealing clothes led viewers to assign less agency to the wearer. These findings dont stop with adults — 5th grade girls wearing sexualized clothing were also seen as less than.

Regan A. R. Gurung, author of 2 of the above studies, suggests that the issue in schools is less with what students wear, and more with the link between their attire and their intelligence or competence. Basically, dress codes are a symptom of a larger societal issue: sexism. And students seem to get that. In an interview, Maggie Sunseri, creator of the documentary Shame: A Documentary on School Dress Code said "It's about the message behind the dress codes. It's not about just superficially what we're wearing, it's about what people are telling us that means about ourselves."

While taking on ages of systemic sexism may seem like a daunting task, working with your local schools to ameliorate the messages in their dress code policies is a lot more manageable. The National Womens Law Center has some suggestions for doing just that. Among them: include policies that maintain gender neutrality, dont remove students from the classroom for dress code violations, and survey the students about how the dress code policies affect them. The Oregon chapter of the National Organization for Women (Oregon NOW) has also created a model dress code policy that they say does not reinforce or increase marginalization or oppression of any group based on race, gender, ethnicity, religion, sexual orientation, household income, gender identity or cultural observance.

Methods • Download the Data Here

All school handbooks were chosen for analysis opportunistically as follows:

Using the National Center for Education Statistics (NCES) search function for public schools, I collected a list of over 8,000 public high schools in the US. I limited the resulting schools to just those that had a web address listed in NCES. Accessing the homepage web content from each site, I searched for words like handbook, dress code, and code of conduct, filtering my list of schools to just those that contain one of the above phrases. Then, I manually visited the resulting (2,000+) websites in an attempt to find the dress code. Dress codes were disqualified from analysis if they: had a uniform policy, were not from the 2018-2019 school year, or represented magnet or boarding schools (according to the NCES).

Once the list of dress codes was compiled, I and two data assistants (Kaitlyn Thomas and Anna Houston) manually recorded every rule listed in each dress code, the words used in the dress codes rationale, as well as any listed sanctions for breaking the dress code. Only items and body parts that were explicitly listed were included in this analysis. Some minor subjectivity came into play when combining similar phrases from different handbooks (ex. midriff shirt = crop top, shirt with open sides = muscle shirt etc.).

Looking for More?

In the larger conversation around school dress codes, the sexualization of students is simply one part. Luckily, some amazing work has been done focusing on this and other areas as well:

Dress Coded: Black girls, bodies, and bias in D.C. schools

National Womens Law Center and DC Students • 2018

Combining data from all but 3 of Washington DCs public high schools written dress code policies alongside the lived experiences of 21 black girls that attend/have recently attended those schools, this report explores the effect of dress code policies on black girls. The authors of this report also discuss the impact of uniform policies on families and suggestions for how educators and policymakers can improve dress codes.

How dress codes criminalize males and sexualize females of color

Alyssa Pavlakis and Rachel Roegman • 2018

Pavlakis and Roegman surveyed an entire schools student population (with a total of 384 responses) and 13 teachers to learn about how often students followed the dress code, were disciplined for breaking the policies, and their feelings about the dress code overall. They explore gender, race, and the intersection of the two.

Undressing the Dress Codes: An analysis of gender in high school dress code policies

Jaymie Arns • 2017

Through their Masters Thesis, Arns dissects the dress codes in 56 public high schools in California (2016-2017 School Year) and how students at the intersections of gender and race are disproportionately targeted.

Tuck in that Shirt! Race, class, gender, and discipline in an urban school

Edward Morris • 2005

Over the course of 2 school years, Morris observed and recorded the race, class, and gender influence on school disciplining of students bodies and dress in a Texas middle school.

Model Student Dress Code

Oregon NOW • 2016

The Oregon National Organization for Women (NOW) created a model student dress code intended to address recent and escalating controversy and conversation both in Oregon and across the nation about overreaching and detrimental dress codes for some K-12 school students.
